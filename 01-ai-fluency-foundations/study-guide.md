## 1. Overview

**Course:** [Anthropic AI Fluency: Framework & Foundations](https://anthropic.skilljar.com/ai-fluency-framework-foundations)  
**Audience:** Everyone — regardless of current AI expertise level  
**Goal:** Make AI Fluency accessible and useful for navigating the evolving landscape of AI collaboration

### What This AI Fluency: Framework & Foundations Is About

It is designed to help you:
- Understand how to collaborate with AI tools **effectively** and **responsibly**
- Develop a durable, framework-based approach that holds up even as new AI models and tools emerge
- Think critically about AI's impact on creative, business, scientific, and educational domains
- Engage with AI in ways that are not just productive, but **ethical** and **safe**

Most introductions to AI tools focus on tips and tricks tied to specific platforms. AI Fluency instead builds **transferable competencies** — skills that remain relevant regardless of which AI system you use or how rapidly the technology evolves.

## 3. The AI Fluency Framework

### What Is AI Fluency?

**AI Fluency** is not just about knowing how to use an AI tool. It is a set of interconnected human competencies that enable you to:

- Decide **when** and **how** to involve AI in your work
- Communicate **clearly and precisely** with AI systems
- **Evaluate** AI outputs critically and thoughtfully
- Act **responsibly and ethically** when using AI in real-world contexts

### Framework Design Philosophy

The framework rests on a key insight: **AI collaboration is a human skill**. Just as writing well requires more than knowing how to type, working well with AI requires more than knowing which buttons to press.

The four competencies are **interconnected** — they reinforce and depend on each other. Strong AI collaboration requires developing all four, not just one or two.

## 4. The Four Competencies

### 4.1 Delegation

> *Knowing **when**, **why**, and **whether** to involve AI in a task.*

#### Core Questions Delegation Answers:
- Should I use AI for this task at all?
- What parts of this task are AI-suited vs. human-suited?
- What are the tradeoffs of delegating this work to an AI?

#### Key Concepts:

**Task Decomposition** — Breaking a complex goal into sub-tasks and identifying which are good candidates for AI involvement. Not everything benefits from AI, and recognizing that distinction is itself a skill.

**Appropriate vs. Inappropriate Delegation** — Some tasks are well-suited for AI (e.g., drafting, summarizing, brainstorming at scale). Others require human judgment, lived experience, or ethical accountability that AI cannot substitute.

**Human-AI Division of Labor** — Understanding your own role when AI is involved. Even when AI does significant work, you remain responsible for the output.

#### Delegation Decision Framework:

| Factor | Consider AI | Keep Human-Led |
|---|---|---|
| Task repetition | Highly repetitive | Unique, one-of-a-kind |
| Stakes of error | Low-stakes draft | High-stakes final decision |
| Creativity type | Volume/variation | Deep originality |
| Ethical weight | Routine | Sensitive or consequential |
| Expertise required | Broad/general | Deep domain knowledge |

#### Learning Objectives for Delegation:
- Identify tasks where AI adds genuine value
- Recognize tasks where AI involvement is inappropriate or risky
- Articulate your reasoning for involving (or not involving) AI
- Understand the concept of human accountability in AI-assisted work

---

### 4.2 Description

> *Knowing **how** to communicate with AI clearly, precisely, and effectively.*

#### Core Questions Description Answers:
- How do I communicate my intent clearly to an AI?
- What information does the AI need to give me useful output?
- How do I structure my requests for best results?

#### Key Concepts:

**Prompting as a Communication Skill** — Writing a prompt is not about magic words; it's about clear communication. The same principles that make human communication effective — clarity, specificity, context — apply to AI interaction.

**Context and Constraints** — AI systems perform better when given relevant background information, the purpose of the task, the intended audience, and any constraints (format, length, tone, etc.).

**Iterative Refinement** — Effective AI collaboration is rarely a single exchange. It's a conversation: you evaluate, refine, redirect, and build toward the output you need.

**Anatomy of an Effective Prompt:**

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│             EFFECTIVE PROMPT ANATOMY         │
│                                              │
│  1. ROLE / CONTEXT                           │
│     "You are a..." / "I am working on..."    │
│                                              │
│  2. TASK                                     │
│     Clear, specific statement of what        │
│     you want the AI to do                    │
│                                              │
│  3. CONSTRAINTS                              │
│     Format, length, tone, audience,          │
│     things to include or avoid               │
│                                              │
│  4. EXAMPLES (when helpful)                  │
│     Sample output or reference material      │
│                                              │
│  5. EVALUATION CRITERIA                      │
│     How will you judge whether it worked?    │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

#### Common Description Pitfalls:
- Being too vague ("write something good about this")
- Overloading a single prompt with too many tasks
- Assuming the AI shares your implicit knowledge or context
- Accepting the first output without iterating

#### Learning Objectives for Description:
- Write clear, structured prompts with sufficient context
- Identify what information an AI needs to complete a task well
- Practice iterative refinement through multi-turn exchanges
- Adapt communication style based on the type of task

### 4.3 Discernment

> *Knowing how to **evaluate**, **verify**, and **critically assess** AI outputs.*

#### Core Questions Discernment Answers:
- Is this AI output accurate, reliable, and appropriate?
- What are the limitations of this output?
- How do I know when to trust vs. question the AI?

#### Key Concepts:

**AI Outputs Are Not Ground Truth** — AI systems can be confidently wrong. They can hallucinate facts, reflect biases present in training data, or produce plausible-sounding but incorrect information.

**Critical Evaluation** — Discernment means engaging actively with AI output rather than passively accepting it. Ask: Does this make sense? Is this consistent with what I know? Can I verify this independently?

**Understanding AI Limitations** — Recognizing the boundaries of what AI systems can reliably do. These include knowledge cutoffs, inability to access real-time data, difficulty with nuanced ethical reasoning, and susceptibility to framing effects.

**Source and Fact Verification** — When AI provides factual claims, especially for high-stakes uses, those claims should be independently verified using trusted primary sources.

#### Discernment Evaluation Checklist:

```
For any significant AI output, ask:

Does this align with my prior knowledge and expertise?
Are any factual claims verifiable? Have I checked them?
Does the output reflect any obvious biases or assumptions?
Is the reasoning sound, or does it just sound plausible?
Would a domain expert agree with this output?
Are there important nuances or caveats missing?
Is this output appropriate for my specific context and audience?
What would go wrong if this output contains errors?
```

#### The Spectrum of AI Reliability:

| Output Type | Reliability Level | Recommended Action |
|---|---|---|
| Creative drafts | Variable | Evaluate quality, revise |
| Factual claims | Medium-Low | Always verify independently |
| Code/logic | Medium | Test and review carefully |
| Reasoning/analysis | Medium | Stress-test the logic |
| Recommendations | Variable | Apply human judgment |
| Predictions | Low | Treat as one input only |

#### Learning Objectives for Discernment:
- Identify common failure modes in AI outputs
- Apply critical evaluation criteria to AI-generated content
- Develop habits of verification for factual claims
- Recognize bias and framing effects in AI responses

### 4.4 Diligence

> *Acting **responsibly**, **ethically**, and **safely** when using AI in real-world contexts.*

#### Core Questions Diligence Answers:
- What are my responsibilities when I use AI?
- Who could be affected by my use of AI, and how?
- How do I use AI in ways that are safe and ethical?

#### Key Concepts:

**Human Accountability** — Using AI does not transfer responsibility. When you use AI to produce work, you remain accountable for that work's accuracy, impact, and appropriateness.

**Transparency and Honesty** — Being honest about AI involvement when it's relevant — with colleagues, clients, readers, or other stakeholders. This includes understanding the disclosure norms in your field or context.

**Societal Impact Awareness** — Understanding that AI use at scale has broader effects: on labor markets, information ecosystems, creative industries, privacy, and equity. Diligence means staying informed and thoughtful about these dimensions.

**Avoiding Harm** — Proactively considering whether your use of AI could cause harm — directly (by producing harmful content) or indirectly (by displacing human expertise, spreading misinformation, or reinforcing discrimination).

**Privacy and Data Responsibility** — Understanding what data you are sharing with AI systems, and exercising appropriate care with sensitive, personal, or proprietary information.

#### Diligence in Practice:

```
BEFORE using AI:
  → Have I considered whether AI is appropriate here?
  → What data am I about to share? Is it sensitive?
  → What are the potential downsides or risks?

WHILE using AI:
  → Am I guiding this toward responsible outputs?
  → Am I checking the AI's work rather than blindly accepting it?

AFTER using AI:
  → Am I being appropriately transparent about AI involvement?
  → Have I taken responsibility for the quality of the final output?
  → Who might be affected by this output, and how?
```

#### Ethical Dimensions of AI Use:

| Dimension | Questions to Ask |
|---|---|
| **Fairness** | Does this AI use treat all people equitably? |
| **Transparency** | Am I being honest about AI's role? |
| **Privacy** | Am I protecting sensitive information? |
| **Accountability** | Am I taking responsibility for the output? |
| **Harm Prevention** | Could this cause direct or indirect harm? |
| **Sustainability** | Is this use of AI contributing positively to society? |

#### Learning Objectives for Diligence:
- Articulate personal and professional responsibilities when using AI
- Apply appropriate transparency norms in your context
- Evaluate AI use through an ethical and societal lens
- Handle sensitive data responsibly in AI-assisted workflows

## 5. Why AI Fluency Matters

### The Multidisciplinary Imperative

Responsible AI development and engagement cannot be addressed by any single discipline or viewpoint. It requires perspectives from:

- **Technical fields** — to understand how AI systems work
- **Creative disciplines** — to explore how AI reshapes expression and authorship
- **Business domains** — to navigate AI's economic and organizational impacts
- **Scientific communities** — to apply rigorous inquiry to AI's capabilities and limits
- **Educational sectors** — to build AI literacy at scale
- **Humanities and social sciences** — to understand AI's cultural and ethical dimensions

The AI Fluency Framework is explicitly **multidisciplinary** — it was co-created by educators spanning art and design (Ringling College) and business and computing (University College Cork) in partnership with an AI safety company (Anthropic).

### AI Fluency Is Durable

Technology changes fast. AI models evolve. New tools emerge constantly. The specific techniques for prompting Claude today may be different from those needed for next year's systems.

But the four competencies — **Delegation, Description, Discernment, Diligence** — are grounded in human judgment and responsibility. They remain relevant regardless of which AI model you're using, because they're about how *you* think and act, not about which buttons to press.

### AI Fluency Is for Everyone

You do not need to be a programmer, data scientist, or AI researcher to develop AI Fluency. This framework is designed for:

- Students across all disciplines
- Professionals in any field
- Educators and researchers
- Artists and creators
- Business leaders and managers
- Anyone navigating AI's presence in daily life

## 6. Core Principles of Responsible AI Collaboration

These principles run through all four competencies and underpin the entire framework:

### 1. Humans Remain Accountable
AI is a tool. When you use it, you are responsible for the outputs, regardless of how good or confident the AI's response appears.

### 2. AI Collaboration Is a Skill
Like writing, public speaking, or critical thinking, effective AI collaboration can be learned, practiced, and improved. It is not purely intuitive.

### 3. Context Always Matters
What counts as appropriate, effective, or ethical AI use depends heavily on context — your field, your audience, the stakes involved, and the norms of your community.

### 4. Critical Thinking Is Non-Negotiable
AI fluency requires active, skeptical engagement with AI outputs — not passive acceptance. The ease and confidence of AI generation is precisely what makes critical evaluation so important.

### 5. Responsible Use Benefits Everyone
When individuals use AI responsibly, they contribute to healthier information ecosystems, fairer outcomes, and safer AI deployment at a societal level.

## 7. Key Takeaways & Summary

### The Big Picture

The AI Fluency Framework gives you a durable, principled approach to human-AI collaboration — one that remains useful regardless of how AI technology evolves.

### The Four Competencies at a Glance

| Competency | Core Idea | Key Question |
|---|---|---|
| **Delegation** | Deciding when and how to involve AI | *Should I use AI for this?* |
| **Description** | Communicating clearly and effectively with AI | *How do I ask for what I need?* |
| **Discernment** | Critically evaluating AI outputs | *Is this output accurate and appropriate?* |
| **Diligence** | Acting responsibly and ethically | *What are my responsibilities here?* |

### The Outcomes These Competencies Enable

Developing all four competencies leads to AI collaboration that is:

- **Effective** — You get outputs that genuinely help you achieve your goals
- **Efficient** — You work with AI in ways that save time and effort without cutting corners
- **Ethical** — You use AI in ways that are honest, fair, and responsible
- **Safe** — You protect yourself, others, and society from AI-related harms

### The Enduring Principle

> *AI Fluency is ultimately a set of human skills — skills of judgment, communication, critical thinking, and ethical reasoning. These do not become obsolete when new AI models appear. They become more important.*
